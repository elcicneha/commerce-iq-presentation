kosha how groups work — the hard question

Groups change. People leave.
Who pays for the wifi bill?

Flatmates move out. Friends join a trip. Groups are never really frozen. Here's how I thought about it.

1 tricky

Track who joined and left — and when.

Sneha left on the 15th, so she won't be charged for the wifi bill that comes on the 20th. Expenses split themselves based on who was in at the time.

Sounds fair — but it's hard to reason about. Nobody remembers the exact date someone moved out.

2 too many groups

Lock the group. New dynamic? New group.

Members can't change. If someone joins or leaves, you create a fresh group. History stays clean — new people, new slate. You can still settle up by person across groups.

But what if you made a mistake? And flatmates change all the time. You'd end up with ten groups for the same flat.

3 what I built

Each expense has its own member list. (The Splitwise way.)

It doesn't matter who joined the group when. Every expense is its own thing — you pick who's splitting it right then. One group for the flat, one for the trip, one for the party. Settle up by person across all of them.

No history to manage. No dates to track. Each expense just knows what it knows.

The group is just a label. The expense is what actually matters.

kosha settle up — by person, not by group

You don't pay
a group back.
You pay a person.

You share a flat with Sneha. You also went on a trip with her. And split Priya's birthday dinner.

That's three different groups — but at the end of the month, you just want to know: what do I owe Sneha, total?

  • One number per person — across every group you share with them
  • Breakdown by group — so you can see exactly where the balance came from
  • One payment to settle all of it — no group-by-group math

12 / What's next

If I had 3 more weeks.

  • Settlement auto-detect. When Aarav pays Sneha back, both banks show matching transactions — kosha could spot it and ask "Was this a settlement?" instead of making anyone log it manually.
  • Track savings goals. Each goal is a plant that slowly grows as and when the amount increases.
  • The proportional split. If Aarav earns 1.5× more, the couple may wanna split by income proportion. Adding that option.
  • Category-wise split. Some expenses might not be shared equally. Some married couples I know, split groveries 50/50 but rent is income-based.
  • Category-wise breakdown view. Detailed expense analytics by category — geared toward Sneha, who wants to see where the money actually goes.
